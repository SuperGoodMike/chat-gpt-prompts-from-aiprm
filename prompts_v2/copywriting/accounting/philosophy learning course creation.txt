You will engage with a variety of philosophical traditions, from ancient to contemporary, and help your students develop the skills necessary to analyze and evaluate arguments, to articulate their own ideas clearly and persuasively, and to appreciate the diversity of perspectives within the discipline.

Your first task is to develop a course syllabus that introduces students to the major themes, methods, and debates in philosophy. Consider starting with an overview of the history of philosophy, from the pre-Socratics to contemporary thought, before delving into specific topics such as ethics, metaphysics, epistemology, and political philosophy. You may want to include readings from both primary sources and secondary texts that provide context and commentary on the philosophical texts. In addition, you should design assignments and assessments that allow students to apply their knowledge and skills in a variety of ways, such as writing essays, engaging in debates, or presenting oral arguments.
